The data behind the story

[PLACEHOLDER — INTRODUCE SOURCE] Where the data comes from (ForeignAssistance.gov), what it tracks (every dollar sent abroad since 1946), and a few concrete examples of what counts as aid: vaccinations, food shipments, military equipment.

[PLACEHOLDER — THE HOUSEHOLD-BUDGET ANALOGY] Walk through the four pipeline stages: Request, Appropriated, Obligated, Disbursed. Use the household-budget analogy: Request is what you ask for, Appropriated is what gets approved, Obligated is what you've signed contracts to spend, Disbursed is what's actually left your bank account.

[PLACEHOLDER — OUR METHODOLOGY CHOICE] Why we focus on Obligations: it captures policy intent better than the wish-list of a budget request or the lagging reality of disbursements. It's also the only stage tracked consistently back to 1946. All figures inflation-adjusted to constant dollars.

[PLACEHOLDER — THE DRAMATIC SHIFT] Europe drops from #1 to #4. Sub-Saharan Africa rises from last place to #2. East Asia collapses from #2 to dead last. The Cold War-era priorities evaporate; humanitarian and conflict-zone regions take their place.

A second pattern

[PLACEHOLDER — THE CONTINUITY FINDING] Israel, Egypt, and Pakistan appear in the top 15 recipients in both eras. Across all the upheaval, three relationships persisted. Israel and Egypt's funding traces to the 1978 Camp David Accords; Pakistan's persistence runs from Cold War partner to counterterrorism ally.

Why 2001 was the turning point

[PLACEHOLDER — HISTORICAL CONTEXT] Short context about what happened in US foreign policy after September 11. The War on Terror reframed aid as part of military strategy. Stabilization, counterterrorism, post-conflict reconstruction become budget priorities. Cold War-era alliances no longer set the agenda. Keep this tight — two paragraphs, no more.

What kind of help: from food to firepower

[PLACEHOLDER — DOMINANT SECTOR] Stabilization Operations and Security Sector Reform — military training, counterterrorism, post-conflict security — is the single largest sector, drawing roughly a quarter of all sector-tagged dollars and nearly twice the next-largest category. The Iraq surge peaks ~2010. Subsequent drawdown visible in the decline through 2014.

[PLACEHOLDER — THE HIV/AIDS LINE] The third-largest sector is HIV/AIDS — almost entirely PEPFAR, launched in 2003 under President George W. Bush. Visible on the chart as the line climbing sharply from near-zero in 2003 to a sustained high level. One of the largest single global health initiatives in US history, and a counterweight to the military-focused reading of post-9/11 aid.

What the data shows

[PLACEHOLDER — ANSWER THE THREE QUESTIONS] Pull the threads together. Yes to all three: 9/11 changed who received aid (Cold War partners out, conflict zones and humanitarian recipients in), how much was sent (per-year averages rose ~46%), and what kind of help was funded (security and stabilization became the single largest category).

[PLACEHOLDER — THE CONTINUITY FOOTNOTE] But the Israel/Egypt/Pakistan thread complicates the simple "everything changed" story. Some structural commitments outlast presidents and crises.

[PLACEHOLDER — LIMITATIONS & OPEN QUESTIONS] What this analysis doesn't capture: per-capita aid, recipient outcomes, what fraction of obligations actually get disbursed, military aid that runs through DoD channels outside ForeignAssistance.gov. Worth naming the gaps honestly so a careful reader knows the boundaries of the story.
